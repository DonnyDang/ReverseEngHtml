Here's what we're following today.

Former Defense Secretary Jim Mattis issued an extraordinary critique of how the White House has handled nationwide unrest. Mattis warned against "militarizing our response" to the protests and said that President Trump has sought to divide Americans. The broadside came on the same day that current Defense Secretary Mark Esper said at a Pentagon briefing that he opposed invoking the Insurrection Act — something Trump had threatened to do in a televised speech on Monday. Read the full text of the retired Marine general’s statement.

The coronavirus pandemic has pushed unemployment to its highest level since the Great Depression, but there are signs that the job market could slowly starting to recover. The Labor Department says another 1.87 million people filed claims for unemployment insurance last week, which is down 249,000 from the previous week.

All four former Minneapolis police officers connected with George Floyd's death now face criminal charges. On Wednesday, charges against Derek Chauvin were upgraded to second-degree murder in addition to earlier charges, and the three other former officers were charged with aiding and abetting murder.

History Lesson

Jazz musicians have always faced systems of discrimination in America. One insidious example was the cabaret card, a form of identification required for any musician to work in a New York nightclub from 1940 to 1967. The New York Police Department administered these licenses and revoked them for any minor infraction. As a result, some of the biggest names in music at the time, like Billie Holiday and Charlie Parker, lost their right to work at crucial moments in their careers. A new Jazz Night in America video short traces the history of the cabaret card from its racist origins to its toll on the music, and reflect on what might have been. Click here to watch the video.
